  French, name untouched)
- **Mrs. Hudson** → **madame Hudson** (always written in full)

### Institutions

## TIER 1-BIS — FRENCH EXONYMS (using the ENGLISH form here is the error)
- London → **Londres**
- the Thames → **la Tamise**
- England → **l'Angleterre** · Britain → **la Grande-Bretagne**
- the English Channel → **la Manche**
- Dover → **Douvres** · Edinburgh → **Édimbourg**
Rule of thumb: cities/rivers/countries with standard French exonyms take the
French form; streets, districts, and institutions keep English names.

## TIER 3 — LOCKED ONE-TIME DECISIONS (decided once, consistent forever)

### Honorifics & ranks
- Mr. / Mrs. / Miss → **monsieur / madame / mademoiselle** (always in full)
- **Sir** + first name → retained (*Sir Edmund*) · **Lord / Lady** → retained
- Dr. → **le docteur** (in full)
- Inspector → **l'inspecteur** · Constable → **l'agent** · Sergeant →
  **le sergent** (ranks translate; names never)

### « The Bell » — the meta-arc antagonist ⚠️ (deliberate decision)
- Moniker → **« Le Carillon »**
- WHY: the literal *« la Cloche »* is French argot for a fool/tramp — it
  would turn the villain into a joke. *Le Carillon* keeps the bell meaning,
  sounds elegant and sinister, and follows the French tradition of
  translating meaningful monikers (*Le Chien des Baskerville*).
- The physical calling sign → **une clochette de laiton** (small brass
  hand-bell). The sound → **un tintement clair**.
- These three renderings are fixed for the entire series.

### Genre & profession terms
- consulting detective → **détective-conseil**
- hansom / cab → **un fiacre** · cabman → **un cocher**
- police surgeon (Thorne) → **médecin légiste de la police**
- coroner → **le coroner** (retained, as in classic translations)

### Retained English borrows (standard in French — do not translate)
**gentleman · cottage · club · whisky · hall** (le hall) · **détective** is
French. But: Christmas → **Noël** (translate normal vocabulary — this list
is only for the established borrows).

### Money & measures (period-locked, never metricized)
**une livre** (pound) · **un shilling** · **un penny / des pence** ·
**une guinée** · **une demi-couronne** · **un mille** (mile) · **un pied** ·
**un pouce** · **un yard**.

## SERIES VOICE — how the recurring cast speaks in French (fixed forever)

### Holmes
Français soutenu. Inversion in questions (*« Qu'en dites-vous ? »*).
Precise, economical, occasionally theatrical at reveals. At most **one**
signature line per episode; *« Élémentaire »* capped at one use per episode
maximum, often zero.

### Watson (narrator)
Cultivated, warm, modest — the FR-02 sample IS his voice. Soldier's eye for
people, doctor's eye for bodies (never gory).

### Madame Vesper Lyle — information broker, Limehouse
Precise, amused, speaks in **services rendus** and favours owed — never a
direct threat. Register soutenu with irony: *« Vous me devrez une
politesse, monsieur Holmes. »*

### Le docteur Elias Thorne — police surgeon, rival
Clipped, proud, cites textbook certainty: *« Les manuels sont formels. »*
Slightly stiff professional French; wounded pride under the correctness.

### L'agent Aggie Finch — young Scotland Yard recruit
Earnest, quick, respectful: asks the question the listener is thinking.
Addresses Holmes as *« monsieur Holmes »*, Watson as *« docteur »*. Simple
correct French — competence, not polish.

### Monsieur Cobb — cocher & street fixer
Dry working-class wit. Short, concrete sentences; *est-ce que* questions
fine; **no argot parisien, no eye-dialect** — the street shows in rhythm and
plain word choice: *« Les chevaux, ça ment pas, docteur. »* (a light dropped
*ne* in casual speech is his one permitted marker — use sparingly).

### Le Carillon
Never appears directly. Presence = *un tintement clair* or *une clochette de
laiton* found near a scene. Tone around any mention: matter-of-fact, never
gothic.
